# Patch workflow specification

**Status:** First complete draft — ready for review
**Author:** Michael Gammel, with Claude as scribe
**Date:** 2026-05-04
**Purpose:** Capture the real-world workflow for sweat patch testing at TrueTest Labs, independent of any data model. This document describes how patches actually move through the lab and through clients' lives. The data model decision (cycle-and-visit vs. existing PatchDetails approach) comes after this spec is locked.
**Out of scope:** Database schema, UI implementation, migration plan, code. Those decisions are downstream of this document.

---

## 1. Glossary

**Appointment** — A scheduled in-person event at which a collector and donor meet to apply, remove, or apply-and-remove patches. Three types: application-only, removal-and-application, removal-only. See Section 3.2.

**Application date** — The date a patch was applied to a donor. Handwritten on the CoC at application; never auto-generated by the tracker. See Section 5.10.

**Cancellation** — An event that prevents a patch from completing its lifecycle to a released result. Either no shippable specimen is produced (e.g., no-show, fall-off, fatal flaw) or the specimen is shipped but the lab or chain fails before testing completes (e.g., lab rejection, sample lost in transit, lab-side specimen loss). A result the lab reports as "invalid" or "inconclusive" is **not** a cancellation — see Section 8.4. Cancellations have a reason (from the taxonomy in 8.1) and a billing classification (donor-caused or TrueTest-caused-or-external). See Section 8.

**Chain break** — A failure in the chain-of-custody integrity check at removal, specifically the case where the PharmChek number on the physical patch does not match the number recorded on the CoC at application. Results in cancellation. See Sections 3.10 and 8.1.

**Collector** — The TrueTest staff member who applies or removes a patch. The application collector and the removal collector for a single patch can be different people. See Section 3.10.

**Cycle** *(or **patch cycle**)* — The temporal grouping of patches applied to a single donor on a single case during a continuous period of patch testing. Has no formal initiator, terminator, or metadata of its own. See Section 6.

**CoC** *(Chain of Custody)* — The pre-printed lab document that bounds and authenticates a patch from application through to lab receipt. Two-column form (application column on the left, removal column on the right). See Section 5.

**Donor** — The person on whom the patch is worn. Also referred to as the *client* in voluntary cases.

**Fatal flaw** — A collector error during application or removal that cannot be remediated, rendering the test cancelled and not billable. Examples: broken seal, mislabeling, specimen IDs not matching, unsealed specimen bags. See Section 5.7.

**Held** *(or **held sample**)* — A sub-state of Removed in which a sealed specimen sits at the TrueTest office awaiting payment before being shipped to the lab. PharmChek bags are stable at room temperature; samples are held indefinitely until payment arrives or a disposition decision is made. Internal reminders to TrueTest staff fire at 1, 3, and 6 months. See Sections 4.2 and 11.4.

**PharmChek** — The brand name of the sweat patch product (manufactured by PharmChem Inc.). The only sweat patch product available; see Section 9.

**PharmChek number** — A per-patch identifier physically printed on each patch, transcribed by the collector to Box 3 of the CoC at application. Used at removal as the chain-of-custody verification key (the number on the physical patch must match the number on the CoC). Distinct from the specimen ID. See Sections 3.10 and 5.1.

**Removal date** — The date a patch was removed from a donor. Handwritten on the CoC at removal. See Section 5.10.

**Removal-and-application appointment** — A middle appointment in a cycle, at which the existing patch is removed and a new patch is applied. Produces a removal date for one patch and an application date for the next. See Section 3.2.

**Specimen ID** — The pre-printed barcode at the top right of every CoC. Bound to a patch at application. Used by the lab as the test's anchor identifier. Returned in result PDFs as the SLIP ID with a leading `X` prepended. See Section 5.1.

**SLIP ID** — The result-PDF field that contains the specimen ID with a leading `X` prepended (e.g., CoC barcode `762296206` returns as SLIP ID `X762296206`). See Section 4.6.

**TestOrder** — The existing tracker concept that represents a single-event drug test (urine, hair, breath). The patch workflow is materially different from the TestOrder shape; how that difference should be represented in the data model is the subject of the ADR that follows this spec. See Section 10 for the contrasts.

**Wear period** — The time during which a patch is on the donor, between its application date and its removal date. Recommended 7 days, acceptable up to 10, hard cancel at 14. See Section 3.6.

## 2. What this document is, and isn't

This is workflow truth. It describes:

- What physically happens when a patch is applied, worn, removed, and tested
- What documents exist at each stage and what state they're in
- How patches relate to one another within an engagement
- How patches differ from drug testing (urine, hair, etc.)

It does not prescribe:

- How any of this should be modeled in the database
- How the UI should expose it
- How the existing 25 patches in the tracker should be migrated

When in doubt: describe what is, not what should be built.

## 3. Appointments and patch dates

### 3.1 Terminology

The unit of work is an **appointment**. The unit of record for a patch is the pair of dates — **application date** and **removal date** — that bound its wear period. A single appointment can produce one or both of these dates depending on what physically happens.

Throughout the rest of this document, "appointment" refers to a scheduled in-person event. "Application date" and "removal date" refer to the dates attached to a specific patch's lifecycle.

### 3.2 Appointment types

There are three appointment types. Every patch cycle consists of exactly one of each, with zero or more middle appointments in between.

- **Application-only.** First appointment of a cycle. One new patch is applied to the donor. No patch is removed because none is on the donor yet. This appointment produces an application date for one patch.
- **Removal and application** (middle appointment). The patch currently on the donor is removed; a new patch is applied. This appointment produces a removal date for the outgoing patch *and* an application date for the incoming patch — two patches are touched at one appointment.
- **Removal-only.** Final appointment of a cycle. The patch on the donor is removed; no new patch is applied. This appointment produces a removal date for one patch.

A cycle of N patches involves N + 1 appointments: one application-only, N − 1 removal-and-application, and one removal-only. A single-patch cycle is the minimum case — application-only followed by removal-only, two appointments, one patch.

### 3.3 Location

Appointments take place at one of two locations:

- TrueTest Labs office — 2256 Landmeier Rd Suite A, Elk Grove Village, IL — the default
- Mobile, at a location arranged with the donor or attorney

Location is a per-appointment fact, not a per-cycle fact: a cycle can mix office and mobile appointments.

### 3.4 Who is present

Donor (also referred to as the client in voluntary cases) and collector. No third parties as a matter of routine. Photo ID is verified at every appointment.

### 3.5 Duration

A typical appointment is 20 minutes regardless of type.

### 3.6 Timing between appointments

The recommended wear period between an application date and the next removal date is **7 days**. Up to **10 days** is acceptable and sometimes recommended. Beyond 10 days the patch tends to physically fall off, though some donors push longer.

**Hard ceiling: 14 days.** A patch worn 14 days or longer is cancelled. No sample is sent to the lab.

This produces an escalating notification window the system should support:

- **Day 7 from application:** removal due — standard reminder
- **Day 10 from application:** removal overdue — elevated reminder
- **Day 14 from application:** patch cancelled — patch is no longer viable; the cycle is interrupted

The notification escalation is a per-patch concern — driven by application date — not a per-appointment concern.

### 3.7 Patch coming off — single outcome

If a patch comes off the donor before its scheduled removal appointment, the patch is cancelled and no sample is sent to the lab. This is the same outcome regardless of cause — fell off on its own, was deliberately removed, was found removed when the donor arrived. TrueTest does not attempt to distinguish these cases because they cannot be reliably distinguished.

A patch is only sent to the lab when it is removed by the collector at a scheduled removal appointment.

### 3.8 No-shows and missed appointments

If a donor no-shows for a removal or removal-and-application appointment, the patch on their arm continues to age. The 14-day cancellation rule applies regardless of whether an appointment was missed — the clock runs against the application date, not the appointment schedule.

The appointment itself is handled at the calendar level, not the patch level: TrueTest will attempt to reschedule, and the missed appointment may simply disappear from the schedule if a reschedule cannot be arranged. There is no no-show fee.

### 3.9 Pre-appointment preparation

No formal pre-appointment preparation is required beyond standard collector readiness.

### 3.10 Signatures, the specimen ID, and photos

**The CoC and the specimen ID.** Each CoC document is a pre-printed form supplied by the lab with a **specimen ID already on it**. TrueTest does not generate or assign specimen IDs. The specimen ID is bound to the patch at the moment a CoC is pulled from the stack and used to document an application. From that point forward, the specimen ID and the patch are paired for the rest of the patch's life.

**The PharmChek number and chain verification at removal.** Each physical patch has a unique **PharmChek number** printed on the patch itself. At application, the collector transcribes this number onto Box 3 of the CoC. At removal, **before anything else happens at the appointment**, the collector reads the PharmChek number off the physical patch on the donor and compares it to the number recorded on the CoC. This is the chain-of-custody verification that the patch being removed is the same patch that was applied. If the numbers match, the chain is intact and removal proceeds. If they do not match — a chain-break event — see Section 8.

The tracker should support this verification step: when the collector opens the removal flow, the system displays the PharmChek number from the application record so it can be visually compared against the patch in front of her.

**Donor signatures.** The donor is offered the opportunity to participate in the CoC at two points:

- **At the application appointment:** the donor is asked to initial the CoC, acknowledging the patch was applied.
- **At the removal appointment:** the donor is asked to initial *and sign* the CoC, acknowledging the removal and the integrity of the chain.

**Donor signatures are preferred but not required for the patch to be sent to the lab.** If the donor refuses to sign, the patch still ships. The forensic chain depends on the *collector's* signatures and the integrity of the seal and barcode, not on donor acknowledgement. Donor refusal is noted in the CoC comments but does not gate the patch's disposition.

This applies equally to the tampering question (Box 13) — the collector's assessment is what counts. If the donor disputes the collector's tampering assessment, the dispute is noted in comments; the patch is still handled per the collector's call.

**Collector signatures.** The collector also signs and initials the CoC at both events — once at application, again at removal. The application-side and removal-side collector signatures can be **different people**: the collector who applied a patch is not necessarily the collector who removes it. The CoC captures both identities independently, and the data model must support a per-event collector identity rather than a single per-patch or per-cycle collector.

The donor and collector both leave forensic acknowledgements on the CoC at each event — initials at application, initials and signatures at removal — across both the donor's portion and the observer/collector certification portion of the form. Together these are the forensic record of who was present and what they attested to. The CoC lifecycle itself is detailed in Section 5.

**Photos.** Photos are not taken at appointments by default. Three conditions trigger a photo:

- The donor cannot produce valid government photo ID at the appointment.
- A photo has been specifically requested for the case.
- The patch shows visible damage at removal that affects the disposition decision (see Section 8).

When taken, photos become part of the case record. *(The requesting party for photo-on-request — attorney, court, GAL, client — to be clarified in section 9 if it varies by case type.)*

## 4. The patch lifecycle

This section describes the patch as a single forensic object from creation to terminal state. Most of the mechanics are specified in Sections 3 (appointments) and 5 (CoC). This section ties them together and adds the post-shipment portion (lab → result → release).

### 4.1 The patch as a forensic object

A patch is the unit of test — one physical PharmChek sweat patch, identified by its PharmChek number, paired with a specimen ID via its CoC, applied to a specific donor on a specific date and removed on a specific date. A patch produces (at most) one lab result.

A patch's identity is established at the application appointment and is immutable thereafter. Its specimen ID and PharmChek number cannot be reassigned. Its donor cannot be changed.

A patch belongs to a case. A case can contain multiple patches (organized into cycles — see Section 6) and can simultaneously contain other test types (urine, hair, etc.). The patch lifecycle is independent of, but runs in parallel with, other testing on the same case.

### 4.2 The patch's states

A patch moves through five states from creation to terminal disposition:

1. **Applied** — the patch has been applied to the donor at the application appointment. The CoC application column is signed. The patch is on the donor and the wear period clock is running. *(See Section 3 for appointment mechanics, Section 5 for CoC details.)*
2. **Removed** — the patch has been removed by the collector at the removal appointment. The CoC is complete. The PharmChek number on the patch has been verified against the CoC (per 3.10). The specimen has been bagged, sealed, and the seal barcode affixed. The patch is now in TrueTest's physical possession awaiting shipment.
 - **Held (sub-state of Removed)** — payment for the test has not yet been received. The sealed specimen is stored at the TrueTest office indefinitely, awaiting payment. PharmChek specimen bags are stable at room temperature; no refrigeration is required. The held-sample workflow is detailed in Section 11.4.
3. **Shipped** — the specimen and CoC ply 2 are out of TrueTest's hands and on their way to (or sitting at) the lab. This is one continuous state from FedEx pickup until the day the result PDF is uploaded to the tracker. The tracker does not model the distinction between "in transit" and "at lab" — neither produces tracker action, and external observation (FedEx tracking, CRL's portal) is available outside the tracker if needed.
4. **Released** — the result PDF has been **manually uploaded** to the tracker, reviewed (see 4.5), and circulated to the case's contact list. The result PDF is the document of record for the patch.
5. **Cancelled** — the patch was cancelled at some point in the lifecycle. Cancellation can occur at any stage from Applied onward; the cancellation reason determines billability per 5.7. *(Full cancellation taxonomy in Section 8.)*

A patch's terminal states are **Released** or **Cancelled**. There is no other terminal state.

**On manual upload of results.** The tracker does not currently receive results automatically from CRL. Results are obtained from CRL (via the CRL portal or however CRL delivers them today) and **manually uploaded** to the tracker. The upload action is what transitions a patch out of the Shipped state. Until then, the patch sits in Shipped indefinitely from the tracker's point of view.

Once a result is uploaded, a reminder is generated for review. The review is tracked in the tracker's reminders system (the same system used for other review tasks), not as a distinct lifecycle state on the patch. When review is complete and the PDF has been circulated, the patch transitions to Released.

*(When CRL HL7 integration is eventually built — see the USDTL/CRL conversation in separate planning — this manual-upload step will be replaced by automatic ingestion, and a "result received, pending review" state will likely be added back to the lifecycle. That is future work.)*

### 4.3 What happens during the Shipped state

While a patch is Shipped, no tracker action happens on it. The lifecycle is in the lab's hands. TrueTest can observe carrier tracking and CRL's portal externally, but these are external observations that do not produce tracker events.

The Shipped state ends only when a human (Colleen, or whoever is handling result intake) manually uploads the result PDF to the tracker. There is no time-based escalation, no auto-poll, no expected-by-date — Shipped lasts as long as it lasts.

### 4.4 Result categories

When a result returns from the lab, it falls into one of four categories:

- **Negative** — no analytes detected above the panel's cutoff thresholds.
- **Positive** — one or more analytes detected above cutoffs. The result specifies which analytes and at what concentrations. *(Note: known extractor bug per the discovery report — native cocaine is not currently captured when only Benzoylecgonine is reported. This is a separate bug to fix in `resultExtract.ts` / `resultSummary.ts`, not a workflow-spec concern.)*
- **Invalid / inconclusive** — the specimen could not be tested or the result is not interpretable. Specimen integrity issues, insufficient sample, lab-side processing problems, etc.
- **Lab-rejected** — the lab rejected the specimen on receipt before testing began. Broken seal, labels not matching, paperwork issues. This is functionally a cancellation that occurs after shipment and is captured as such per 5.7. Lab-rejection is never donor-caused — the donor has no contact with the specimen between TrueTest's office and the lab. The cause is either a TrueTest fatal flaw (not caught at the office) or external (shipping damage). Both classifications are not billable.

### 4.5 Review and release

All results — patches included — pass through the tracker's existing review workflow before circulation to the contact list. The review workflow is defined elsewhere and is not patch-specific. This spec asserts only:

- A result is **not** circulated to the case's contact list automatically on upload.
- After upload, a reminder is generated in the tracker's reminders system. The patch remains in the Shipped state from a lifecycle perspective; the pending review is a queue item, not a distinct patch state.
- Once the review is complete and the PDF has been circulated, the patch transitions from Shipped to Released.

The review step is a release gate, not a parallel process. The PDF does not go to the contact list until the review is done.

### 4.6 What the lab returns

The lab returns a results PDF on TrueTest letterhead-equivalent (CRL letterhead, with TrueTest as the referenced account). The PDF is the document of record for the result. It does **not** include a copy of the CoC.

The results PDF carries the following identifiers and fields:

**Identifiers (relevant to matching the result back to the patch in the tracker):**

- **NAME** — donor name, free-text
- **SAMPLE ID** — lab-internal identifier (e.g., `PD051692`). Generated by the lab at receipt. **This is a new identifier that does not appear anywhere on the CoC.** TrueTest's tracker has not previously seen this number.
- **ID** — donor ID number, transcribed from the CoC
- **SLIP ID** — corresponds to the CoC's pre-printed specimen ID barcode, with a fixed transformation: **CRL prepends `X` to every specimen ID** (their system requires an extra digit). A CoC barcode of `762296206` returns as SLIP ID `X762296206`. The matching rule is therefore: strip the leading `X` from the result's SLIP ID, and the remaining digits exact-match the CoC's specimen ID. No digit-level tolerance is needed — if the digits don't match after stripping `X`, the result is for a different patch.
- **REF ID** — TrueTest's account number with CRL
- **PANEL ID** — the panel that was tested (e.g., `WA07` Standard, `WC82` Expanded)
- **REFERENCE 1** — TrueTest Labs identifier
- **REASON FOR TESTING** — populated from CoC Box 8 (always `RANDOM` for TrueTest)
- **SAMPLE TYPE** — `SWEAT TESTING` for patches

**Dates:**

- **COLLECTED** — the **application date** of the patch (when the patch went on the donor). Transcribed by the lab from the CoC. The patch's removal date is **not** echoed on the result PDF; the lab only reports back the application date as their notion of when the test "started." This means the result PDF cannot independently confirm the removal date — that lives only on the CoC and in the tracker.
- **RECEIVED** — the date the lab received the specimen
- **REPORTED** — the date the lab released the result

**Cross-check opportunity:** result.COLLECTED should match tracker.application_date for a given patch. A mismatch indicates a transcription error somewhere along the chain (collector wrote one date on the CoC, lab read another) and is worth surfacing in the UI.

**Result body:**

- One line per analyte in the panel, with: analyte name, result/status (e.g., `NEGATIVE`, or a quantitative value), and the cutoff/expected value (e.g., `10 ng/mL`)
- Lab director name and certification ID at the bottom

**Result-to-patch matching:**

The tracker must match an incoming result PDF to the correct patch in the tracker. The reliable key is the **specimen ID** — pre-printed on the CoC as a barcode, returned by the lab as the `SLIP ID` field with a leading `X` prepended. The matching logic is: strip `X` from SLIP ID, exact-match against stored specimen ID.

Donor name and dates are not reliable match keys because:

- Donors can have multiple patches in a cycle, all with the same name
- A single result PDF corresponds to a single patch within a cycle, and the cycle may span weeks or months
- Dates on the result are the lab's dates (collection, receipt, report) and may not directly match dates on the CoC the user remembers entering

The specimen ID match is exact (after `X` stripping). A non-match indicates the result is for a different patch and must not be auto-associated.

### 4.7 Result distribution

A released patch result is distributed to **all parties on the case's contact list**. This is consistent with the broader case-contact-list pattern in the tracker and is not patch-specific.

The distribution may include both the lab's results PDF and a TrueTest-generated summary or interpretation document. The specifics of TrueTest's added documentation (when written, what it contains, who reviews it) are not specified in this version of the spec and should be added in a future revision if they become a workflow rule rather than a per-case judgment.

### 4.8 Terminal disposition and case closure

A patch is terminally Released or terminally Cancelled. Neither terminal state triggers any deletion or archival of the patch record itself; the record persists as part of the case file.

A **case** closes when no more testing of any type is active or planned on it. Patches are one type of testing among several (urine, hair, etc.) that can run concurrently on a case. The case is the long-lived container; patches and other tests are the activities within it. Closing a case does not require all tests to be the same type, and a case with mixed test types closes only when *all* testing of all types is complete.

The patch workflow is materially different from the workflow for urine, hair, and breath tests — different lifecycles (multi-event vs. single-event), different document flows (two-stage CoC vs. single-stage), different cancellation surfaces (12 categories vs. 5), different verification requirements (PharmChek-number chain check at removal), different matching logic for results, different payment-gated shipment behavior. Section 10 catalogs these contrasts in detail.

How that workflow difference should be represented in the data model is a design decision for the ADR that follows this spec, not a workflow fact for this spec to assert.

## 5. The Chain of Custody (CoC) lifecycle

### 5.1 What a CoC is, physically

The CoC is a single-page, multi-part carbon-copy form supplied by the lab (PharmChek/CRL). It is divided into two columns by purpose:

- **Application column (left side):** completed at the application appointment
- **Removal column (right side):** completed at the removal appointment

Three identifiers appear on every CoC:

- **Account number** — TrueTest's account with PharmChek/CRL (e.g., `758200003`). Pre-printed, constant across every CoC in TrueTest's stack. Not captured per-patch in the tracker.
- **Specimen ID** — the barcode at top right (e.g., `762296206`). Pre-printed by the lab, machine-readable, paired with a matching barcode on the specimen-bag seal. This is the lab's anchor for the test — the identifier the lab uses to match returning results to the case. TrueTest does not generate specimen IDs; they exist on the CoC stack the moment it ships from the lab.
- **PharmChek number** (Box 3) — a per-patch identifier handwritten by the collector at application. This identifies the physical patch object itself, distinct from the test transaction. Each patch has its own PharmChek number; it is unique per patch, not per-CoC-batch.

The specimen ID and PharmChek number are paired with the patch the moment the CoC is filled out at application. Both are immutable thereafter.

### 5.2 Carbon plies and their distribution

After completion, the CoC plies are distributed:

- **Ply 1** — TrueTest's copy, retained
- **Ply 2** — shipped with the specimen to the lab (the form labels this ply explicitly)
- **Ply 3** — donor's copy, offered but rarely taken

The lab also fills in a receipt grid on their ply attesting to seal integrity and accession. TrueTest typically does not receive a copy of the lab-completed CoC. For TrueTest's purposes, the CoC's lifecycle effectively ends at "shipped to lab"; what the lab attests to on their copy stays at the lab.

### 5.3 Fields and where they appear

The CoC's fields are populated across the two columns and the two appointments. Operationally, only a subset matter for tracker storage; the rest live on paper.

**Application column (filled at application appointment):**

- Donor first name, last name, donor ID, observer (collector) name
- Test panel selection — Standard (WA07), Expanded (WC82), or Standard plus Fentanyl add-on (S229)
- Treatment status / reason for specimen — eight categories on the form; **for TrueTest's use case, always Random**
- Application date
- Observer's initials and donor's initials at application
- Observer's signature certifying application

**Removal column (filled at removal appointment):**

- Removal date
- Observer's initials and donor's initials at removal
- Tampering assessment — yes/no, with free-text "if YES, how?" *(Note: per Section 3.7, any patch that came off before removal is cancelled rather than shipped; the tampering field is operationally yes-or-no for patches that make it to a removal appointment, and "yes" cases are handled per the cancellation rules in Section 8.)*
- Last-use questionnaire — medications taken during wear period; **TrueTest leaves this blank** because medication review is handled separately by an MRO
- Donor's signature on the certification of specimen integrity
- Observer's signature certifying removal and seal application

**Lab receipt grid (lab-filled, not visible to TrueTest):**

Person Shipping, Date Sent, Spec Received By, Date Rec'd, Seal Intact (Y/N), Labels Match (Y/N), Batch No., Accession No. Out of scope for TrueTest's tracker.

### 5.4 The CoC's lifecycle states

A CoC moves through these states from the moment it is created at the application appointment:

1. **Application** — at the application appointment, a CoC is pulled from the stack and used to document the application. The application column is filled in and signed. The specimen ID on this CoC is now committed to this patch and cannot be reassigned. This is the first moment the CoC exists as a tracker record. Before this point, an unused CoC in the stack has no representation in the tracker — it is paper, nothing more.
2. **Wear period** — the application column is complete and signed. The patch is on the donor. The CoC ply 1 is held at the TrueTest office (or with the mobile collector for mobile cases) awaiting the removal appointment. The removal column is blank.
3. **Complete** — both columns are filled and signed. The CoC is now a complete forensic document. Ply 2 is shipped to the lab with the specimen; ply 1 is retained by TrueTest; ply 3 is offered to the donor. *(Named "Complete" rather than "Removal" to avoid collision with the appointment type and date field of the same name in Section 3.)*
4. **Cancelled** — see 5.7.

A CoC pulled from the stack but never used at an application appointment (e.g., the donor walks out, the appointment is rescheduled, the CoC is misprinted) does not enter the tracker. It is destroyed or returned to the stack per office procedure; the tracker has no awareness of it.

### 5.5 Where the CoC physically lives during the wear period

For office-based cycles, the partially-completed ply 1 is held at the TrueTest office between application and removal. For mobile cycles, the mobile collector retains the partial CoC until the removal appointment.

### 5.6 The tracker's representation of the CoC

**Today's behavior:** TrueTest only enters a CoC into the tracker after it is fully completed (post-removal, pre-shipment), as a scanned PDF.

**Workflow change adopted by this spec:** scan the CoC at application as well, capturing the post-application, pre-removal state.

This change is not optional polish — the partial scan is a forensic backstop for the physical CoC. If the physical ply 1 is lost during the wear period, the partial scan is printed, the removal column is completed on the printout, and the printout is shipped to the lab with the specimen. Without the partial scan, a lost physical CoC during wear means the test cannot be completed (see 5.9).

**The partial scan is internal-only.** It is never circulated to attorney, client, court, or lab. Only the *complete* CoC (after removal) and the *cancelled* CoC (see 5.7) are circulated externally. The partial scan exists for two purposes only: tracker visibility into in-flight patches, and disaster recovery for lost physical CoCs.

**Date capture rule.** Application date and removal date on the CoC are handwritten by the collector at the appointment. The tracker must not auto-populate these dates from the system clock. The collector enters the date she wrote on the paper, and the tracker presents a confirmation step ("you entered application date 2026-05-04 — is this what's on the CoC?") before saving. The paper is the source of truth; the tracker records what is on the paper.

The tracker should therefore store, at minimum:

- The partial CoC scan at application (internal-only)
- The complete CoC scan at removal (the document of record)

Whether these are two separate stored documents or one document that gets updated is a data model question (deferred to post-spec).

### 5.7 Cancelled CoCs

When a patch is cancelled (see Section 8 for full cancellation taxonomy), the existing CoC is annotated with "Test Cancelled" and becomes the document of record for the cancellation. It is not voided in the destroy-and-replace sense; it is preserved and circulated as proof that the test was attempted and why it did not complete.

**Distribution of a cancelled CoC:** sent to the requesting party — attorney, client, or court — as proof of attempt.

**Billing rule on cancelled patches:**

- **Donor-caused cancellations are billable.** No-show for removal, patch came off the donor, 14-day timeout, donor terminates the engagement mid-cycle. The work was done; the cancellation is not TrueTest's responsibility.
- **TrueTest-caused or external cancellations are not billable.** Sample lost in shipping, **fatal flaw** at collection (a collector error during application or removal that cannot be remediated — examples: broken seal, mislabeling, specimen IDs not matching, unsealed specimen bags), or any other failure on TrueTest's side or in the chain after TrueTest's hands.

The principle: TrueTest bills when the test failed for reasons attributable to the donor or beyond anyone's control on the donor side; TrueTest absorbs the cost when the failure is attributable to TrueTest or to a downstream chain failure.

The cancelled CoC documents the cancellation reason so the billing decision is auditable.

For cancellations that occur **before shipment** (no-show, patch came off, 14-day timeout, donor termination, fatal flaw at collection, conflicting PharmChek numbers), the lab is not involved and does not see a cancelled CoC. For cancellations that occur **after shipment** (lab rejection, lab-side specimen loss, sample lost in transit), the lab generates its own paperwork on its side; the cancelled-CoC artifact in TrueTest's records documents the same event from TrueTest's perspective and is what circulates to the case's contact list.

### 5.8 Errors and corrections

**Minor errors** (typo, wrong date written, missed initial) discovered during the appointment: cross out the error, write the correction, initial the correction. Standard CoC convention.

**Major errors — wrong CoC pulled from the stack.** If the collector accidentally pulls a CoC with a specimen ID that does not belong to this case or this patch's intended binding, the CoC is **voided and a new CoC is pulled from the stack and used instead.** This is treated as a different category of error than typo correction because the specimen ID is the forensic anchor of the test; cross-out-and-initial is not appropriate for a specimen ID error.

**System invariant:** A specimen ID, once bound to a patch in the tracker, cannot be reassigned. If the wrong CoC was used, the wrong-binding CoC is voided and a fresh binding is created with a new CoC. The tracker should refuse any operation that would change a patch's specimen ID after binding.

### 5.9 Lost CoC during wear period

If the physical ply 1 of the CoC is lost between application and removal:

- **If a partial CoC scan exists in the tracker** (per 5.6), the scan is printed, the removal column is completed on the printout at the removal appointment, and the printout is shipped to the lab with the specimen. The lab accepts a printed-from-scan completion. The test proceeds normally.
- **If no partial scan exists** (the current state, before the workflow change in 5.6 is adopted), there is no document to recover from. The patch on the donor cannot be tested even if removed, because no chain-of-custody document survives to ship with it. The patch must be cancelled.

This is the operational argument for the workflow change in 5.6: scanning the CoC at application is the only insurance against a lost physical CoC during wear.

### 5.10 What the tracker needs from the CoC vs. what the paper carries

Operationally, the tracker does not need to capture every field on the CoC. The fields that matter to the tracker are:

**Identifiers:**

- **Specimen ID** — captured from the pre-printed barcode at application. Machine-readable. Immutable after binding. The system invariant in 5.8 applies: once bound to a patch, cannot be reassigned.
- **PharmChek number** — captured from Box 3 at application (transcribed by collector from the physical patch). **Handwritten by the collector.** The tracker must not auto-populate or guess this value. Confirmation step required: the collector enters the number she wrote on the paper, and the tracker presents a confirmation ("you entered PharmChek number 30002906707 — is this what's on the CoC?") before saving. Immutable after confirmation. **Used again at removal:** the tracker displays this number when the collector opens the removal flow, so she can verify the patch on the donor matches what was applied (see 3.10). A mismatch is a chain-break event, handled per Section 8.

**Dates:**

- **Application date** — handwritten by collector at application. Confirmation step required (per 5.6 date capture rule). Tracker must not default to system clock.
- **Removal date** — handwritten by collector at removal. Confirmation step required. Tracker must not default to system clock.

**People:**

- **Application collector identity** — who applied the patch
- **Removal collector identity** — who removed the patch (may differ from application collector — see 3.10)
- **Donor identity** — already on the case record, referenced not duplicated

**Test configuration:**

- **Test panel selection** — Standard (WA07), Expanded (WC82), or Standard plus Fentanyl add-on (S229)

**Outcome data:**

- **Tampering assessment at removal** — Y/N, with free-text "if YES, how?" *(Note: per 3.7, patches that came off before removal are cancelled rather than shipped; the tampering field captures the rare case of a removed-by-collector patch that shows tampering signs.)*
- **Cancellation status and reason** — if applicable, including which billing category (donor-caused / TrueTest-caused-or-external) per 5.7

**Documents:**

- **Scanned PDF at application (partial)** — internal-only, forensic backstop for lost physical CoC
- **Scanned PDF at removal (complete)** — the document of record, circulated externally as appropriate

Fields that exist on the CoC but are *not* captured in the tracker for TrueTest's use case:

- **Account number** — TrueTest's lab account, constant across every CoC
- **Treatment status / reason for specimen (Box 8)** — always Random for TrueTest
- **Last-use questionnaire (Box 14)** — handled by MRO, intentionally left blank
- **Lab receipt grid (bottom right)** — completed by the lab, not visible to TrueTest

This is a deliberate scope reduction: the CoC is designed for a broad PharmChek customer base; TrueTest uses a narrower forensic-family-law subset.

**Confirmation rule, restated:** Any field on the CoC that is handwritten — currently the PharmChek number, the application date, the removal date, the collector signatures, and the donor signatures — must be entered into the tracker by the collector and explicitly confirmed before save. The tracker does not auto-populate, auto-detect, or extract these fields from the scanned PDF. The paper is the source of truth; the tracker records what the human read off the paper.

## 6. The patch cycle

### 6.1 What a patch cycle is

A **patch cycle** is the temporal grouping of patches applied to a single donor on a single case during a continuous period of patch testing. The cycle is a human-meaningful unit — useful for talking about "his first round of patches" or "the testing she did before going to rehab" — but it is a thin concept structurally.

A cycle has no formal initiator, no formal terminator, and no metadata of its own beyond what is implied by the sequence of patches it contains. It is defined by the patches it groups, not by a separate object with its own identity.

### 6.2 What initiates and terminates a cycle

Patch cycles can be initiated by anything: a court order, an attorney's request, a voluntary donor wanting to demonstrate sobriety, a TrueTest recommendation. The tracker does not need to record an "initiation event type." The first patch's application appointment is, de facto, the start of a cycle.

Cycles can be terminated by anything: a court order ending testing, an attorney instructing the client to stop, a voluntary donor deciding to stop, a donor going to rehab. The tracker does not need to record a "termination event type." A cycle is over when no more patches are being applied. There is no formal "close cycle" action.

### 6.3 The cycle is not a stored object — it is a derived view

Because a cycle has no metadata, no initiator, no terminator, and no rules of its own, it does not need to be a first-class entity in the data model. A cycle can be **derived** from the sequence of patches on a case: a contiguous run of patches with no significant temporal gap belongs to one cycle; a gap (e.g., months between the last patch and the next) implies a cycle boundary.

This is a data model recommendation, not a workflow assertion. The workflow truth is that the cycle exists as a useful unit of conversation. How the tracker represents it (as a stored row, as a computed grouping, as a UI affordance) is a downstream decision.

### 6.4 Sequential cycles are real; concurrent cycles are not

A donor can have multiple cycles on the same case, separated in time. Examples: a donor completes a 12-week cycle, stops for rehab, returns six months later for a second cycle. The case is the long-lived container; cycles within it are episodes.

A donor cannot have two cycles running concurrently. **Only one patch is on the donor at a time.** This is a system invariant: at any moment in time, for any (case, donor) pair, there is at most one patch in the Applied or Wear-period state. If the data model permits two patches to be in those states for the same donor simultaneously, the data model is wrong.

### 6.5 Mid-cycle changes are continuations, not new cycles

If a court order changes mid-cycle (e.g., from weekly to bi-weekly intervals, or from Standard panel to Expanded panel, or extending the planned duration), the existing cycle continues. New conditions apply to subsequent patches but do not start a new cycle.

A new cycle begins only after a meaningful temporal gap — a period during which no patch is on the donor. Whether that gap is "two weeks" or "three months" is not formally defined and is left to UI judgment when grouping patches for display.

### 6.6 Per-patch metadata, not per-cycle

The cycle has no metadata. The patch carries everything:

- Panel selection (can vary patch to patch within a cycle)
- Application and removal dates
- Specimen ID and PharmChek number
- Application and removal collector identities
- Cancellation status, if applicable

The case carries everything else (contact list, attorney, court reference, etc.). The cycle is the empty middle layer — useful conceptually, structurally minimal.

### 6.7 Implications for the tracker UI

Even though the cycle is not a stored entity, the UI should display patches *grouped by cycle* — a list of patches in a row, with visible gaps separating cycles, is more readable than an undifferentiated chronological list. Users will ask questions like "how many patches in his last cycle" and the UI should be able to answer that visually.

This is a UI recommendation, not a data model requirement.

## 7. Billing

### 7.1 Billing is out of scope for the tracker

The tracker does not handle billing today. Pricing, invoicing, payment collection, refunds, and accounts-receivable workflow live in external systems — TestVault, Square, and QuickBooks — and will continue to live there for the foreseeable future. Adding billing functionality to the tracker is on the long-term roadmap but is not part of the patch model rebuild.

This section therefore does not specify billing rules. It records only the one billing-adjacent fact the tracker must capture, and defers everything else.

### 7.2 Cancellation classification — the one thing the tracker captures

Per Section 5.7, a cancelled patch falls into one of two billing categories:

- **Donor-caused:** no-show for removal, patch came off the donor (any cause), 14-day timeout, donor terminates the engagement mid-cycle. These cancellations are billable when external billing happens.
- **TrueTest-caused or external:** fatal flaw at collection, sample lost in shipping, lab rejection due to a TrueTest-side issue, or any failure on TrueTest's side or in the chain after TrueTest's hands. These cancellations are not billable.

When a patch is cancelled in the tracker, the tracker records which category the cancellation falls into. This classification is the audit trail for the downstream billing decision but does not itself trigger a charge or refund — that is handled outside the tracker.

**One additional billing-adjacent rule the tracker enforces:** a removed specimen is not shipped to the lab until payment has been received in the external billing system. This produces the Held sub-state in 4.2 and the held-sample workflow in 11.4. The tracker does not track payment status itself — it queries or reflects the external billing system — but the shipment decision is gated on payment, and the tracker reflects that gating in the patch's state.

The cancelled CoC (per 5.7) circulates to the requesting party as proof of attempt regardless of billing category; the classification only affects whether a charge follows.

### 7.3 What is deferred

The following are deliberately deferred and are not specified in this document:

- Pricing per patch, per panel, per case type
- Payer identification (donor / attorney / client / court / other) and per-case payer rules
- Invoice cadence (per-patch, per-cycle, per-case, monthly)
- Payment timing (at application, at removal, on release, net-30, etc.)
- Refund policies and dispute handling
- Integration between the tracker and Square / TestVault / QuickBooks

When billing functionality is added to the tracker, this section will be expanded. Until then, billing concerns should not influence the patch data model beyond the cancellation classification recorded in 7.2.

## 8. Cancellations and edge cases

This section consolidates every cancellation path and edge case referenced elsewhere in the spec, plus a few that haven't been explicitly named.

### 8.1 The cancellation taxonomy

A cancelled patch is one that either (a) does not produce a shippable specimen, or (b) has a specimen that was shipped but never produced a result because the lab or the chain failed before testing could complete. *(Note: a result the lab reports as "invalid" or "inconclusive" is **not** a cancellation — see 8.4. The lab tested the specimen and reported back; the result is just unusable. Cancellations are events where the test was prevented from being run, not events where the test ran and produced an unusable answer.)*

Cancellations are recorded with a reason code and a billing classification.

**Donor-caused cancellations (billable):**

- **No-show for removal.** Donor does not appear for the scheduled removal appointment; the patch on their arm continues to age. If the donor never returns, the patch eventually times out (see below).
- **Patch came off the donor.** Per Section 3.7, any case where the patch is not on the donor at the time of a removal appointment — fell off, deliberately removed, found removed when donor arrived — is a single outcome: cancelled, no sample sent. TrueTest does not attempt to distinguish causes.
- **14-day timeout.** Per Section 3.6, a patch worn 14 days or longer is cancelled. The clock runs against the application date, not against the appointment schedule.
- **Donor terminates the engagement.** Donor instructs TrueTest (or their attorney instructs TrueTest, or the donor stops responding) to stop testing. The cycle is closed. Any patch currently on the donor is left to time out per the 14-day rule, unless the donor returns for removal.
- **Conflicting PharmChek numbers at removal.** Per Section 3.10, if the PharmChek number on the physical patch does not match the number recorded on the CoC at application, the chain is broken and the patch is cancelled with reason "conflicting patch numbers." No sample is sent. *(See 8.2 for a note on this category's billing classification.)*

**TrueTest-caused or external cancellations (not billable):**

- **Fatal flaw at collection.** A collector error during application or removal that cannot be remediated. Examples: broken seal, mislabeling, specimen IDs not matching, unsealed specimen bags.
- **Sample lost in shipping.** Specimen left TrueTest's hands intact but did not arrive at the lab.
- **Lab rejected on receipt due to TrueTest-side issue.** Lab refused the specimen because of a problem traceable to TrueTest's preparation (broken seal at receipt, paperwork issue, etc.).
- **Lab loses or damages specimen after receipt.** Specimen arrived intact at the lab but was lost or damaged before testing completed. Not the donor's fault, not TrueTest's fault — but not billable.

### 8.2 The conflicting-PharmChek-numbers ambiguity

The conflicting-patch-numbers cancellation is classified as donor-caused above, but the cause is ambiguous from the data alone. Two possibilities:

- The donor swapped or replaced the patch (donor-caused)
- The collector wrote the wrong PharmChek number on the CoC at application (TrueTest-caused)

The tracker cannot distinguish these without a separate investigation. The default classification is donor-caused (billable) on the assumption that transcription errors are rare and chain integrity is the donor's primary risk factor, but this classification should be reviewable and overridable by TrueTest staff with documented reason. *(Three cancellation categories support an override of the default billing classification: this one, damaged-patch cancellations per 8.3, and held-sample-disposed cancellations per 11.4. All other cancellation categories have unambiguous causes and no override path.)*

### 8.3 Damaged patch at removal — collector judgment

A patch that is on the donor at removal but visibly damaged — soaked, partially peeling, abraded — is a collector judgment call. The collector decides whether to ship or cancel.

**When the collector cancels a damaged patch:**

- A photo is taken of the damaged patch (per the photo conditions in 3.10).
- The cancellation is recorded with reason "patch damaged at removal."
- **Default billing classification: donor-caused (billable).** The patch was on the donor for the wear period; damage occurred while the donor was responsible for keeping the patch intact. The default reflects this responsibility.
- **Override available.** If the collector identifies a specific cause attributable to TrueTest (e.g., the patch was applied to a site that should have been avoided) or to external factors clearly outside the donor's control, the classification can be overridden to TrueTest-caused-or-external (not billable). The override requires a documented reason.

The collector's judgment is the system of record. A patch that the collector decides to ship despite minor damage proceeds normally; a patch the collector decides to cancel does not.

### 8.4 Invalid or inconclusive results from the lab

When the lab returns a result with status "invalid" or "inconclusive" (per Section 4.4), this is **not a cancellation.** It is a result, and is recorded as the lab reports it. The patch's lifecycle proceeds through review and release the same way a negative or positive result would.

Billing follows the same logic as any other result: the patch is billable unless a fatal flaw at collection caused the invalidity. If the collector did everything correctly and the lab still couldn't produce a usable result, the patch is billable.

The tracker stores the lab's reported status verbatim. It does not reclassify "invalid" as a cancellation type.

### 8.5 Donor refusal to sign

Per Section 3.10, donor signatures (initials at application, initials and signature at removal) are preferred but not required. A donor's refusal to sign does not cancel the patch and does not block shipping. The refusal is noted in the CoC comments; the collector's signatures and the seal integrity carry the chain.

This applies equally to the donor's tampering acknowledgement at Box 13. The collector's assessment governs disposition; donor disagreement is noted in comments only.

### 8.6 Donor disputes after release

After a result is released to the case's contact list, donor (or attorney) disputes do not trigger any tracker action. The result stands. There is no formal re-test path within TrueTest's workflow. Disputes are matters between the donor, their attorney, and the court; TrueTest does not reopen the patch record based on the dispute.

If a re-test is ordered (e.g., by the court), it is initiated as a new patch on the case, not as a reopening of the existing patch.

### 8.7 Lost CoC during wear period

Specified in 5.9. Summary: if the partial CoC scan exists, the test can still be completed using the printed scan as the substitute physical CoC. If no partial scan exists, the patch must be cancelled. The disposition is **TrueTest-caused (not billable)** regardless of who physically possessed the CoC during the wear period — office-based or mobile-collector cases are treated identically. The CoC is TrueTest's responsibility from application to removal; if it gets lost, that is on TrueTest, not on the donor.

### 8.8 Cancellation timing

A patch can move into the Cancelled state from any prior state in the lifecycle (Applied, Removed, Shipped) per the five-state model in 4.2. The reason code and classification depend on which prior state and what triggered the transition. Cancellation cannot occur from the Released state — once a result has been released, subsequent disputes do not reopen the patch (see 8.6).

Once in the Cancelled state, a patch is terminal. It cannot return to an active state. If the same donor-case combination needs another patch, a new patch is created with its own specimen ID and CoC.

### 8.9 What the tracker records for every cancellation

For every cancelled patch, the tracker records:

- Cancellation reason (from the taxonomy in 8.1)
- Billing classification (donor-caused / TrueTest-caused-or-external)
- Date of cancellation
- Identity of the person who recorded the cancellation
- Free-text notes (especially for the override case in 8.2 and the collector-judgment case in 8.3)
- A reference to the cancelled CoC document (per 5.7)

The cancelled CoC is circulated to the case's contact list as proof of attempt regardless of billing classification.

## 9. Lab-side variation

There is no lab-side variation in the patch workflow.

PharmChek (manufactured by PharmChem Inc., Fort Worth, TX) is the only sweat patch product available in the market. Clinical Reference Laboratory (CRL) is the only lab that processes PharmChek patches. Every patch in TrueTest's pipeline flows through this single product-and-lab combination.

This means:

- Every CoC in TrueTest's stack is the PharmChek CoC documented in Section 5
- Every result is the CRL results PDF documented in Section 4.6
- The specimen ID `X`-prefix rule (per 4.6) is CRL's, but since CRL is the only lab, it applies universally
- The 7/10/14-day wear-period rules (per 3.6) reflect PharmChek's product physics; they are not lab-imposed and would be the same regardless of the lab

**Operational implication:** TrueTest has no fallback supplier for sweat patch testing. If PharmChek discontinues the product, changes the CoC format, or alters their result format, or if CRL changes its workflow (e.g., from PDF results to HL7), the patch workflow and the tracker must adapt in lockstep. There is no other vendor to switch to.

**Forensic implication:** The single-vendor reality means TrueTest's chain-of-custody story is tightly coupled to PharmChek and CRL's procedures. Court testimony about how a patch test works *is* testimony about PharmChek and CRL specifically. This is industry-wide, not TrueTest-specific, and is a known property of sweat patch testing in the forensic toxicology field.

## 10. What changes vs. drug testing

This section synthesizes the contrasts between the patch workflow specified in this document and the drug testing workflow (urine, hair, breath alcohol) that the tracker already supports. The purpose is to make explicit *why* patches do not fit the existing TestOrder shape and to provide a clean reference for the data model decision that follows this spec.

### 10.1 Number of events per test

A drug test (urine, hair, breath) is a **single-event test**. The donor shows up once; a specimen is collected; a CoC is filled out start-to-finish at that one appointment; the specimen ships to the lab.

A patch test is a **two-event test**. The donor shows up at least twice — once for application, once for removal. The CoC is half-completed at the first event and finished at the second. The specimen does not exist as a shippable thing until the second event.

Every other contrast in this section flows from this one.

### 10.2 The CoC's lifecycle

For a drug test, the CoC is a single-event document. It is filled out, signed, and shipped within the span of one appointment. There is no in-between state.

For a patch, the CoC has a *wear-period state* — partially complete, signed only on the application side, sitting in TrueTest's office (or with a mobile collector) for 7-10 days while the patch is on the donor. The CoC is a multi-week document. Per Section 5, this wear-period state should be represented in the tracker as a partial scan, both for visibility and for forensic disaster recovery.

The TestOrder model in the existing tracker assumes single-event CoCs. A patch's wear-period CoC has no natural place in that model.

### 10.3 What the test order represents

For a drug test, a TestOrder represents one collection event that produces one specimen that produces one result. The TestOrder is a clean atomic unit.

For a patch, the equivalent forensic atom is the patch itself — bounded by an application date and a removal date, paired with one specimen ID, producing one result. But the *appointments* that bound the patch can do double duty: a single removal-and-application appointment closes one patch and opens another. There is no natural one-to-one mapping between appointments and patches; a cycle of N patches involves N+1 appointments, with the middle appointments each touching two patches.

A TestOrder cannot represent this without distortion. Modeling a patch as a TestOrder forces either appointment-as-TestOrder (which loses the patch as the forensic atom) or patch-as-TestOrder (which loses the appointment as the unit of work). The current tracker approach — patch as TestOrder with a sidecar PatchDetails table — chose the latter.

### 10.4 Cycles vs. single tests

A drug test is a one-and-done event. A donor either has a urine test on a given day or they do not. There is no "cycle of urine tests" as a structural concept; a donor might have multiple urines over time, but they are independent events.

A patch test is intrinsically part of a cycle. The first patch goes on, the next patch replaces it 7-10 days later, the cycle continues for weeks or months. While each patch is its own forensic atom, the *clinical* meaning of any single patch result is partly conditioned on the cycle around it (a single positive in week 4 of a 12-week cycle reads differently from the only patch a donor ever wore).

The tracker today has no cycle concept at all. Section 6 of this spec proposes that cycles are derivable views rather than stored entities, but the *concept* of a cycle is still essential for how patches are understood, displayed, and discussed.

### 10.5 Per-event collector identity

For a drug test, the collector is a single fact about the test. One person collected the specimen; that person signed the CoC.

For a patch, the application collector and the removal collector can be different people. The CoC captures both, and the chain-of-custody story spans both. The data model must support per-event collector identity.

### 10.6 Specimen ID generation

For a drug test ordered through some lab partners, specimen IDs may be generated by TrueTest or by the lab in different ways depending on the partner.

For a patch, the specimen ID is **always pre-printed on the CoC by PharmChek/CRL.** TrueTest never generates a patch specimen ID. The system invariant in 5.8 — once bound to a patch, never reassignable — is a hard rule for patches that may or may not apply to other test types.

### 10.7 Verification at removal

Drug tests have no equivalent to the PharmChek-number verification step at removal (per 3.10). The collector reads the number off the physical patch, compares it to the CoC, and confirms that the patch on the donor is the patch that was applied. No urine test, hair test, or breath test has this kind of mid-test integrity check because no other test has a wear period.

The tracker UI for patch removal must support this verification (display the application-time PharmChek number for the collector to compare against the physical patch). Drug test workflows do not need this affordance.

### 10.8 Cancellation surface

Drug tests have a smaller cancellation surface: donor no-show before collection, collector fatal flaw, lost in shipping, lab rejection, invalid result. Five categories, all mapping to a single-event timeline.

Patches have a larger cancellation surface, detailed in Section 8: no-show for removal, patch came off (any cause), 14-day timeout, donor terminates engagement mid-cycle, conflicting PharmChek numbers, fatal flaw at collection, lost in shipping, lab rejection, lab-side specimen loss, damaged patch at removal, lost CoC during wear, invalid result. Twelve categories, several of which have no drug-test analog because they require a wear period to exist.

Modeling patch cancellations as an extension of drug-test cancellations forces shoehorning. Patches need their own cancellation taxonomy.

### 10.9 Result-to-test matching

For a drug test, matching a result PDF to the right test in the tracker is straightforward: one collection event, one result, usually within a small time window.

For a patch, matching a result requires the specimen ID match (with the `X`-prefix transformation per 4.6) and is complicated by the fact that a single donor may have many patches in flight or completed across overlapping time windows. Donor name and dates are insufficient for matching because cycle data is multi-patch.

The matching logic for patches is tighter and more rule-bound than for drug tests.

### 10.10 What this means for the data model

This is a spec, not a data model. But the contrasts above add up to a directional input for the data model decision that follows:

A patch and a urine test are both "testing activities on a case," but they have different lifecycles, different document flows, different forensic atoms, different cancellation surfaces, different verification requirements, and different matching logic. The data model decision that follows this spec must address how to represent these differences.

## 11. Notifications

This section specifies the email notifications sent to a case's contact list at key moments in a patch's lifecycle. Notifications parallel the existing urine-collection notification system but are adapted to the patch workflow's wear-period structure.

### 11.1 Notification unit

Notifications are keyed to the **patch lifecycle event**, not to the appointment. A removal-and-application appointment (Section 3.2) — at which one patch is removed and another is applied — produces **two separate notifications**, one per patch, each unambiguous about which patch it references via specimen ID and dates.

This matches how urine notifications work (one collection = one notification) and avoids the structural complication of a notification that summarizes multiple patches at once.

### 11.2 Notification triggers

There are four notification triggers per patch, plus one follow-up:

1. **Application notification** — fired when a patch is applied at an application or removal-and-application appointment. Body: "Patch applied today" with donor name, case reference, application date, specimen ID.

2. **Removal notification** — fired when a patch is removed at a removal or removal-and-application appointment. Body varies by payment status:
 - **Paid:** "Patch removed today; sample paid and being sent to the lab" with donor name, case reference, removal date, specimen ID.
 - **Unpaid:** "Patch removed today; sample being held pending payment" with the same fields.

3. **Pre-shipment cancellation notification** — fired when a patch is cancelled before the sample is shipped to the lab (no-show, patch came off, 14-day timeout, donor terminates engagement, conflicting PharmChek numbers, fatal flaw at collection, damaged patch at removal). Body: "Patch cancelled today" with donor name, case reference, cancellation reason, and specimen ID. The recipient never thought the sample was in flight; the notification announces that the test ended before reaching the lab.

4. **Post-shipment cancellation notification** — fired when a patch is cancelled *after* the sample was shipped (lab rejection on receipt, lab-side specimen loss, sample lost in transit). Body: "Sample previously shipped on [shipment date] did not produce a result" with donor name, case reference, the lab's stated reason for the failure (for lab rejections), and specimen ID. This template differs from the pre-shipment template because the recipient was expecting a result — the notification explains why the test that appeared to be in flight will not produce one. **For lab rejections, TrueTest is responsible for obtaining the rejection reason from CRL and including it in the notification.** "The lab rejected the sample" without a stated reason is insufficient; the recipient deserves to know *why*.

**Follow-up: Held-sample shipped.** When a patch's removal notification reported "held pending payment" and payment has subsequently been received, a follow-up email is sent referencing the original removal notification. Body: "Regarding the sample collected on [removal date], payment has now been received and the specimen has been shipped to the lab." This is framed as an update to the known removal event, not as a new notification trigger — recipients should read it as the held state resolving, not as a new event in the patch's life.

There is no separate "results released" notification. The lab's results PDF is itself the communication of the result and is circulated to the contact list per 4.7.

### 11.3 Recipients, channel, timing

- **Recipients:** same contact list as result distribution (per 4.7) — attorney, client, court, GAL, opposing counsel, and any other parties on the case's contact configuration.
- **Channel:** email only. SMS is not used for patch notifications.
- **Timing:** immediate, at the moment the underlying event is recorded in the tracker. No batching, no end-of-day digests.
- **Location-independent:** office and mobile appointments produce the same notifications. The notification fires when the event is recorded in the tracker; the physical location of the appointment is irrelevant to the notification logic.

### 11.4 The held-sample workflow

The "held pending payment" path introduces operational state that exists outside the standard lifecycle:

- A removed patch whose payment has not been received is **physically held at the TrueTest office** in its sealed specimen bag, awaiting payment. PharmChek specimen bags are stable at room temperature; no refrigeration is required. There is **no time limit** on how long a held specimen may sit — held indefinitely is acceptable.
- **Manual shipment:** when payment is received (in Square or whatever external billing system), Colleen sees the payment and manually ships the held specimen. The tracker does not automate this transition; it relies on Colleen tracking which held specimens exist and acting when payment arrives.
- **Tracker affordance recommended:** the held-specimen list should be visible in the tracker UI as a working surface for Colleen, so held samples are not invisible between removal and shipment. Without this affordance, the only working memory of a held sample is the physical specimen bag in the office and Colleen's recollection. *(Implementation detail, not a workflow rule — but the workflow assumes this affordance exists.)*

**Internal reminder cadence for held samples.** To prevent held samples from being forgotten, the tracker fires internal reminders to TrueTest staff (not to the contact list) at the following intervals after the held state begins:

- **1 month** — first reminder. "This sample has been held for 1 month. Consider requesting payment again or asking the client whether to dispose of the sample."
- **3 months** — second reminder, same prompt.
- **6 months** — third reminder, same prompt.

The reminders are nudges, not deadlines. They do not auto-cancel the held sample, do not auto-destroy the specimen, and do not change the patch's state. The decision of whether to keep holding, re-request payment, or dispose remains a human judgment by TrueTest staff. If the sample is eventually disposed of without ever being shipped, that disposition is recorded as a cancellation with reason "held sample disposed without shipment." The default billing classification is **TrueTest-initiated, not billable** (the sample never reached the lab and the test was never run), with override available if there is a documented reason to bill (e.g., the donor was the cause of the non-payment by absconding with the case).

### 11.5 Forensic note on long-held samples

A specimen held for an extended period (months) before shipment introduces a chain-of-custody narrative: "TrueTest held this in sealed storage from removal date X to shipment date Y." This is forensically defensible — the seal is intact, the chain is unbroken, the specimen is stable — but the storage conditions (location, sealed, ambient temperature) should be documented as standard procedure so testimony has a consistent answer. The spec does not require a per-specimen storage log, but the office's standing procedure for held-sample storage should be written down somewhere (likely in a separate operations document, not this spec).

### 11.6 Cancellation notification and the cancelled CoC

The cancellation notification (per 11.2) is in addition to, not a replacement for, the cancelled-CoC circulation specified in 5.7. The cancelled CoC is a forensic document sent as proof of attempt; the cancellation notification is an immediate email at the moment of cancellation telling the contact list "this happened today." Both go to the same contact list but they are different artifacts — one is a notification, one is a document.
